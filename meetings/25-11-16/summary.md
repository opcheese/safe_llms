## Context
Follow-up to Nov 2 meeting. Students have started working on AI safety topics. Kirill has shared documents on AI dependency/harm assessment.

## Topics Discussed

### 1. User Profiling & Questionnaire Design
- Working through a diagnostic questionnaire for detecting harmful AI dependency
- Example questions: frequency of AI use, whether users discuss critical decisions with real people, whether they believe AI gives definitive answers, financial impact
- Challenge: contradictory/control questions (like in military psych tests) to detect dishonesty
- Sonya started writing user personas in different "zones" (safe, gray, critical)

### 2. Kirill as "Persona #3" — A Gray Zone Case Study
Kirill presented himself as a real test case to show the complexity of the gray zone:
- Expert-level AI knowledge, uses AI 12-18 hours/week (60-80+ when actively coding)
- Writing a game design book using AI ("vibe writing"), considers it the best book ever
- Actively promotes it to colleagues despite no external validation
- Considering increasing AI spending from $20/month to $100-1000/month
- Gets genuine joy from the process, no obvious life harm, but shows some concerning patterns (overconfidence, promotion behavior)
- Key question: is this pathological or just an enthusiast?
- **Sonya's verdict:** probably fine, no critical diagnosis
- **Misha's verdict:** hard to say
- Lesson: the boundary between normal and pathological is a massive gray area. Most users will fall in the gray zone.

### 3. Diagnostic Criteria Discussion
Two competing approaches discussed:
- **Questionnaire-based scoring** (like psychological tests) — threshold of checkmarks/crosses, weighted questions. Problem: arbitrary weights, easy to game.
- **Financial/material harm measurement** — based on "disposable income" percentage spent on AI. More concrete: 10% of disposable income = normal hobby; spent all disposable income + quit job + wife left = clearly pathological.
- Kirill prefers concrete harm metrics over subjective questionnaires but acknowledges both have value

### 4. Ethical Boundaries
- Misha proposed having the AI deliberately provoke users to reveal their true state (like a stress test)
- Kirill rejected this for the thesis scope — too ethically dangerous, comparable to drug manufacturing
- Profiling emotions is illegal in EU under AI Act — legal dimension to consider

### 5. Opioid Crisis Analogy
- Kirill drew parallels to the US opioid crisis: stigma prevents people from admitting dependency, making treatment harder
- AI dependency should be treated as a health issue, not a moral failing
- Any person can be vulnerable regardless of intelligence

### 6. Brief: BCI and Direct Brain Measurement
- Misha mentioned brain-computer interfaces (EEG headsets) as a potential diagnostic tool
- Kirill: interesting but the problem is urgent now, BCI mainstream adoption is decades away

## Decisions
- Questionnaire approach + financial harm metrics both worth exploring
- Will NOT pursue the "provocation testing" approach (too ethically risky)
- Sonya to create additional personas: someone healthy + someone in the "gray zone" (not just critical cases)
